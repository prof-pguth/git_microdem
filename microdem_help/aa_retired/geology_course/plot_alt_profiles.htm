Plot Altimeter Profiles

  • A single orbit will have up to about 2700 readings from the TOPEX altimeter; the number depends on how much of the orbit is over land.
  • If you are looking at the medium wavelength geoid anomaly for plate tectonic features, you want the profile to be as short as possible to show the feature you want, as otherwise the very long wavelength anomalies will swamp the display.
  • In general you would like the profiles to run perpendicular to the features.  In this case you only have two choices, an ascending or a descending orbit. You can however select another feature, or another location along the feature, to get a better geometry.
  • If you have a previous subset, remove it with:
    • Zoom out to show entire map data.
    • Table view, All recs to show all the altimeter data.
  • Subset & zoom to get just the region of interest
  • Table view, Map query, and then either:
    • "Current map area" to filter the database.
    •   Rectangular region: Outline region on the map, and show all records in the box.
  • To find the orbit you want try one of these methods:
    • Identify data base records; choice on either the Table view or map toolbar. Double click on the map, and then get the orbit, and then Filter based on the orbit.
      • Table view, Stats, 2D graphs, 2D graph, 2 y-axes.  This puts both the geoid anomaly and the depth on the same graph, even though they have very different scaling.  As shown in the diagrams below, you can use distance, latitude, or longitude for the x axis. 
      • Long straight sections on the line will be missing data where the altimeter was over land
  • Graph modification
    • zoom in to a particular range.
    • Change the symbol colors, or turn off symbols
    • Change the line color, or turn off the line drawing
  • File, Close, Close all graphs can get rid of all the profile graphs quickly.
  • Measuring the anomalies
These three graphs show the same data.  Because of the curvature of the orbits, the shapes appear very different.  This is an ascending orbit, so all three are in the same order.

Plot by distance in the orbit.  In general this will have the most consistent scaling.

  • For an ascending (odd numbered) orbit, 0 will be at the south end of the orbit.
  • For a descending (even numbered) orbit, 0 will be at the north end of the orbit.
