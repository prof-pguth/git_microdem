Airborne Lidar System and Processing

The airborned lidar system consists of:

  • A scanning laser, typically operating at several thousand scans per second (kilohertz).
    • Airborne topographic mapping lidars generally use 1064 nm (NIR) diode pumped YAG lasers.  These lasers are invisible to the human eye and do not attract attention.
    • Bathymetric systems generally use 532 nm (green) frequency doubled diode pumped YAG lasers because green light  penetrates water with much less attenuation than does NIR which is almost completely absorbed.
    • A common alternative, 1550 nm lasers, are eye-safe at much higher power levels  and are not visible in night vision goggles.
  • A GPS unit and an IMU/INU, inertial mapping/navigation unit.  These have complimentary capabilities, the GPS giving a new location about every second, but subject to noise, and the IMU proving a smoother, more realistic path.  The IMU/INU also captures the pitch, roll, and yaw of the aircraft, so the system knows the exact orientation of each scan.
  • The lidar instrument typically scans up to about 20 degrees from either side of nadir, and records the orienation of the lidar beam as it leaves the instrument.
  • Electronics and storage to control the instruments and record the data.
  • Many systems also have a camera, either RGB or RGB/NIR, to record the scene for later merging into the lidar data.
  • The instrument can record the full waveform (intensity of energy returned versus TWTT), or pick out the distinct peaks as the data is collected. Even if the full waveform is recorded, it is rarely distributed becasue the files are huge and most users do not need them.
  • Data from the instrument will be recorded in a vendor specific format.

Processing, generally done by a contractor.  Some of the final steps could be done by the national mapping agency prior to hosting the data on the web.

  • The raw data is rarely delivered to the customer.
  • The vendor merges the navigation data, sensor orientation, and the returns, to find the 3D coordinates of every return.  From the position and orientation of the instrument when the pulse is emitted, and the TWTT to find the distance between the sensor the the point causing the return, the 3D coordinates can be computed.
  • The intensity of the return (how much energy at the laser's waverlength returns).  This looks like a grayscale image, but it will be either green or NIR, which will influence interpretaton. They are not scaled consistently, which presents challenges to display software to provide good contrast.
  • The vendor performs some point classification.  How much depends on the desires of the customer.  Most data sets will have ground returns identified, since they are used to create DTMs.
  • The vendor selects a datum/projection for the data.  This most often in UTM in WGS84 or a local datum like NAD83 or ETRS89 that is functionally equivalent, but can be another local equivalent if required by local law, as in some states in the US where everything must use SPCS.  Hopefully this will be encoded in the files, but some files will not have it and users will have to manually enter it.
  • The vendor tiles the data, for more efficent use, almost always in the open LAS format.
  • The vendor generally compresses the data, most often with LAZ format, but sometimes only with ZIP.
  • The vendor may create DTM/DSM grids, which are significantly smaller in size and can be easier to use.

Distribution if freely avaible

  • Generally the national mapping agency, like USGS in the US, but there is also data from NOAA
  • OpenTopography originally had NSF-funded data for science projects, but now had data from USGS and New Zealand.
  • Many European countries have data, often of higher resolution that most in the US, and often with better point classification. 

End users

  • Manipulate and display the data, either by downloading it on ineracting with it online.
