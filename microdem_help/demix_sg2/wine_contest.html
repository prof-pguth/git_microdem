DEMIX

The current key results from DEMIX:
  • Use FathomDEM if you can use its license
  • Free licenese, use Copernicus DEM, or maybe AW3D30 for very steep terrain 
  • Retire ASTER, SRTM, TanDEM-X, and NASADEM already
  • Do not use GEDTM v1.2

 

 The Committee on Earth Observation Satellites (CEOS),  Working Group on Calibration and Validation (WGCV),   Terrain Mapping Subgroup (TMSG) set up a  Digital Elevation Model Inter-comparison eXercise (DEMIX) (the DEMIX web page might not be very current) to develop open and transparent tools to compare and rank DEMs based on variable criteria.

During  discussions and development, the DEMIX group referred to their methodology as a “wine contest” as an analogy to a comparison of wines by human judges, but with DEMs instead of wines, and both qualitative and subjective criteria instead of judges.  Some reviewers did not like the analogy, and the final paper (Bielski and others, 2024) downplayed  the wine contest analogy.  A follow on paper (Guth and others, 2024) showed the difficulty of using qualitative and subjective criteria at scale for hundreds or thousands of test areas, and that quantitative evaluations were more useful than rankings.  The best evaluations have a common scale, from 0 (best) to 1 (worst). The "wine contest" is a nice analogy, but not very applicable as an accuracte description of the best way to compare DEMs.

MICRODEM has been used for recent papers comparing the global 1 arc second global DEMs:
  • Guth, P.L.; and others 2025 (others being Trevisani, S.; Grohmann, C.H.; Lindsay, J.B.; Reuter, H.I. ) Benchmarking Elevation Plus Land Surface Parameters Finds FathomDEM and Copernicus DEM Win as Best Global DEMs. Remote Sens. 2025, 17, 3919. https://doi.org/10.3390/rs17233919
  • Guth and others, 2024.  This paper has an order of magnitude more test DEMs, and does not use the wine contest statistics.  The revised methodology produces better results compared with Bielski and others, 2024.  While this paper was started after acceptance of the first paper, it had much smoother sailing through the review process.  While this has many of the same authors, it did not formally go through the informal DEMIX group.
  • Bielski and others, 2024.  This paper wanted to call it a "wine contest" to emphasize the statistics could work with both quantitative and qualitative criteria.  After reviewer pushback, the term wine contest was downplayed.
  • Guth and Geoffroy, 2021: used lidar point clouds to rank the global DEMs, and had one of the first assessments of the superiority of the Copernicus DEM.
