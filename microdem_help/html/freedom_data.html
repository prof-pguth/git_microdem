Freedom of GIS Data

Freedom for GIS data consists of two parts:

  • Cost
  • Security restrictions
Image of the White House in Washington DC, from a USGS web site in the mid 2000's.  It appears that someone had outlined the White House and the two office buildings to the east and west, and the censored images were posted.  At the same time, other sites, both commercial and government, had uncensored imagery.

Similar masking, at about the same time, occured at the French submarine base in Brittany.  The French geoPORTAIL blocked the base, but the early Google Maps showed clear details at the same time.

Because GIS data (and the paper map products that existed before the digital age)  has obvious military value, some countries restrict its availability.  In some cases this leads to local problems, when disaster relief from civilian agencies after floods, earthquakes, or hurricanes does not have access to accurate and up to date map information.  In the early days of GIS, only the superpowers had the ability to launch satellites and monitor other countries, and their own.  If one viewed the enemy as the other superpower, they would presumably have satellite observations, but other countries, and your own citizens, would not.  Because the number of satellites was small, and their orbits precisely tracked, sensitive operations could be planned to only take place when no satellites were overhead.  This was not hard, as a satellite pass takes at most about 10 minutes, and you cannot get more than a few looks per day.

It was a big deal when the US Government allowed satellite imagery of Dreamland, or Area 51, or Groom Lake, to be made public.  For a number of people, that is the home of alien flying saucers, while most people think there are just experimental spy satellites there.  For either group, the imagery revealed the number and size of the hangers, and the length of the runways, which allows inferences about about the number of aircraft (or flying saucers) and their size.

In the current environment, it is basically impossible to hide anything from space, and an increasing number of countries have satellites, and commercial firms as well.  There have been attempts to restrict the resolution of commecial satellites, but that only moves the market to other countries, and probably increases the cost of government spy satellites (which must obey the same laws of physics as the commercial ones) becasue the companies have a restricted market.

Goverment map agencies acquire GIS data with public funds.  Some countries have attempted to recoup their costs by selling the data, usually without selling much.  When the data becomes free, usage skyrockets, and the resulting commercial use may in fact bring in more revenue through economic growth and increased tax revenue.  The US is probably the leader in free GIS data, both for the US internally, and for data sets with international coverage like SRTM and Landsat.
