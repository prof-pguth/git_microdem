Fundamentals of Radar Altimeters

Instruments on radar altimeter:

  • Altimeter
  • Navigation systems
  • Radiometer to meter water vapor and correct the altimeter

 

Altimeter fundamentals

  • Must measure 1 m difference from 800 km up (for oceanography), somewhat larger for geoid (now measuring to a few cm)
  • At speed of light, this requires very precise timing
  • For accuracy required, several corrections to the speed of light must be included.
  • Altimeter is an active sensor.

Older radar altimeters typically did not collect over land, because except for the continental ice sheets in Greenland and Antarctica the rough land surfaces scatters too much of the signal and prevents a stong return. Newer altimeters are imporvoing in this regard.

Three basic measurements, all over the oceans:

Geoid height: measure time of travel (actually the TWTT, two way travel, down from the satellite to the sea surface and then back up), and use speed of light (c) to get distance from satellite (Dist = TWTT/2 * c), whose orbit is known. This is generally expressed as an anomaly above or below the reference ellipsoid.

This is not very noisy, since the geoid changes on a much longer time scale than the 10 day orbital cycle of the altimeters.
SWH (Significant wave height): Measure duration of return: if  short, sea state is low and all energy comes back at the same time.

The global map is somewhat noisy because it takes 10 days to collect, and adjacent orbits are collected on different days and storms can dissipate between the collections, but it still gives a reasonable picture.

It clearly shows the largest waves in the southern ocean driven by the West Wind Drift.
Wind speed: Measure strength of return (backscattered power): strong return if low wind; as wind increases, the roughness of the sea surface increases, and less energy is returned because it is reflected in other directions.

This is also somewhat noisy because it takes 10 days to collect, but shows the southern ocean and the West Wind Drift.

Marine geoid

Temporary disturbances of sea level include:

  • Atmosphere
  • Eddies, meanders
  • Major currents

Average many orbits to get geoid, which removes weather and ocean currents.

Subtract average to get oceanographic features like eddies and the wall of the Gulf Stream
