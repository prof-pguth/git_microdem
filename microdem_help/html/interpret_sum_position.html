Interpreting Sun Position

The observer is positioned at the center of the diagram, with the small cross.  Imagine lying on the ground and looking up at the sky.

To find the azimuth to the sun, use the radial lines.  North is at the top, east to the right.  In this diagram the lines are 15 degrees apart.

The elevation of the sun is measured from the outer edge of the diagram, which is 0 degrees and the sun would be right on the horizon.

The point at the center of the diagram has an elevation of 90 degrees (directly overhead), and does not have an azimuth (or has all azimuths).

The hours are shown.  0 and 12 might not be directly north/south, since the location might not be in the center of the time zone.  They would be directly north/south for local noon.

The positions for three locations are shown in brown.

 

This diagram is for a location on the Arctic circle.

  • The blue circle is the June solstice (summer solstice in the northern hemisphere).  At midnight the sun is just tangent to the horizon on the north, and at noon it rises to its maximum altitude of 45 degrees.
  • The red curve is for a specific day, about midway between the June solstice and the equinoxes.  The sun follows this path on two days, in about April and August.
  • The green curve is for the two equinoxes, when the sun rises directly east and sets directly west, and there is exactly 12 hours of daylight.
  • The December solstice curve is missing, because that day has no sunlight at the Arctic circle.
