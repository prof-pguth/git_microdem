Map Projections for Raster& Lidar Data Data

Raster data such as satellite imagery or DEMs is stored in a rectangular grid, with the coordinates of one corner and the spacing in the x and y directions.  The coordinates for every pixel can be computed from the four stored values, and requires essentially no storage.  In contrast a point cloud must explicitly stored the x, y, and z values for every point.

The definition of the stored coordinates uses a map projection, which is a series of equations going between latitude and longitude and cartesian x and y coordinates.  Since it is impossible to take the surface and flatten it on a flat plane, all projections involve compromise.  Computer mapping uses three main types of projection:

  • UTM, the Universal Transverse Mercator system developed by the US military.  This creates 60 UTM zones, each 6 degrees of longtitude, and within the zone there is virtually no distortion.  Computations are very easy, and the only real challenge is dealing with the discontinuities at the UTM zone boundaries.  The Sentinel-2 and Landsat data all use UTM coordinates for their grids.  The maximum distortion in the UTM projection, if you stay within the zone, is about 4 parts in 10,000 (stretching by a factor of about 1.0004).
  • Geographic equidistant cylindrical or equirectangular projection, equidirectional projection,  geographic projection, plate carrée or carte parallelogrammatique projection.  This is not really a projection, but just an assumption the latitude and longitude can be plotted as if they were cartesian coordinates, ignoring earth curvature.  It blows up completely at the poles, where the poles maps to the top and bottom rows of the grid.  This has been used for data because it is "seamless", meaning there are no problems merging data.  Within small areas, the pixels can be considered to be rectangular, and the computations are not much more complicated that with UTM projections.  This is used for all the Global Regional DEMs, but they should not be displayed in this projection.
  • Other projections are used much less commonly.
    • Polar stereographic for the poles.
    • Lambert conformal conic, for regions that are wider than they are tall, such as covering all of the continental US or countries like France or Morocco.
    • Transverse Mercator: rather than using the arbitrary central longitudes used in the UTM, this uses a central longitude centered on the region of interest, which should be taller than it is wide.

The US has a system called the state plane coordinate system (SPCS) , which is designed to have even less distortion that with the UTM system.  To achieve this, each state has at least one zone, and the largest states have more than one, for a total of about 120 zones.  To further complicate matter, many SPCS data sets are in feet, and there are definitions of feet--how you truncate the irrational number matters, and when the coordinates get into the millions, the tiny difference adds up and displaces features.  You should avoid the SPCS system if at all possible (some states require that it be used for legal surveys).
