Glossary

We are no longer trying very hard to keep this current, and some of these definitions are a little tongue in cheek.  We suggest you try http://lmgtfy.com/ (Google or Wikepedia for advanced users) if you need a definition.

  • 8088: CPU in the original IBM PC (very similar to 8086).
  • 80286: CPU in the IBM AT and clones.
  • 80386: CPU in once fast MS-DOS microcomputers.
  • 80486: faster than 80386, initially with built in 80x87.
  • 80586: latest Intel chip, now called Pentium so the 80686 can be the Sexium.
  • 80x87: Intel numerical (math) coprocessor for real number arithmetic. Can be 8087, 80287, or 80387. For 40486, you need to check if the CPU is DX (good) or SX (bad). Necessary for many operations in MICRODEM.  Hard to believe, but you used to have to pay extra for this.
  • ADRG: Arc Digitized Raster Graphics, NIMA scanned maps.
  • ASCII: American standard code for information interchange, which produces files that can be read. In contrast binary files produce more compact storage for numbers or executable code. If a file contains happy faces and other unrecognizable characters, it probably isn't ASCII. Standard ASCII covers 128 characters; IBM introduced a standard for a "higher" set of 128 characters that includes graphics, Greek symbols, and other useful characters (like #248, the degree symbol °). The "high ASCII" characters may not show up as expected on all monitors or printers, including some of IBM's own (like the CGA).  Now there is UNICODE.
  • AVHRR: 5 band (VIS, IR, 3 thermal) instrument on NOAA polar orbiting satellite.
  • Binary: format used internally by the computer, with 8-bit bytes having values 0-255. In this format files cannot be printed or listed on the screen; if attempted, the screen will fill with "happy faces" and other wonderful characters. Binary format results in more compact storage for numbers than ASCII.
  • Borland Pascal: (archaic) whatever happened to poor Turbo Pascal? A better marketing name or something.
  • CADRG: Compressed Arc Digitized Raster Graphics, NIMA scanned maps.
  • CD: compact disk, optical storage for hundreds of megabytes.
  • CGA: (very archaic) Color Graphics adapter, which allows 4 colors with 320x200 resolution. Now pretty obsolete.
  • CGL: Computer Graphics Laboratory, US Military Academy, the father of microcomputer DEM analysis.
  • CIB: Controlled Image Base, a NIMA standard produce with compressed black and white imagery.
  • Column: runs up and down (vertical) in a data set; the number of columns measure the horizontal (across, or east-west) extent of a data set.
  • CPU: central processing unit, the brain of a computer.
  • Datum: constants for shape of the earth. Since all maps don't use the same one, and especially because NAD84 is replacing NAD27, there will be problems with coordinates "moving" up to 100 m in the U.S.
  • Delphi: the latest incarnation of Turbo and Borland Pascal. Makes programming for Windows bearable, and introduced us to databases.
  • DEM: Digital elevation model.
  • DMA: (archaic) Defense Mapping Agency, creator of digital cartographic data for DOD, now know as NIMA.
  • DNAG: Decade of North American Geology, the celebration of the centennial of the Geological Society of America, which produced a number of gridded geophysical data sets.
  • DOD: Department of Defense.
  • DPI: dots per inch. Printers have not gone metric.
  • DS/DD: double sided, double density. 5.25" disks with 360 kb capacity.
  • DTED: Digital terrain elevation data; DMA's name for the 1:250K DEM.
  • DTM: digital terrain model.
  • EGA: (archaic) enhanced graphics adapter, with 16 colors and 640 x 350 resolution.
  • EMS: (archaic) Expanded Memory Specification. A way to address memory above 640 kb; the DEM can be loaded here. A software driver must be loaded; DOS 5.0 handles this for you with EMM386.EXE.
  • Epson: (archaic) printer manufacturer whose dot matrix graphics standards are emulated by most other printers.
  • EROS: Earth Resource Observation Satellite, an early name for Landsat. There is still an EROS Data Center in South Dakota, and the name is used for some of MICRODEM's satellite source code. It makes computers sound erotic.
  • ETL: (archaic) Engineer Topographic Laboratories, at Fort Belvoir, VA, the Army's research lab for computer mapping. Now know as TEC.
  • Flippy: cute little 3½" disks.
  • GAC: Global Area Coverage, resampled 4 km resolution data from the AVHRR.
  • GIGO: Garbage In, Garbage Out
  • GNA: Geophysics of North America, CD-ROM from NGDC, NOAA.
  • GPS: Global Positioning System, satellite navigation with accuracies better than 100 m.  This was with Selective Availability deliberately degrading things; now 5 m or better is probably the case.
  • GUI: Graphical User Interface.
  • HD: high density floppy 5.25" disk with 1.2 Mb capacity, or 3½" disk with 1.44 Mb capacity.
  • IBM: International Business Machines.
  • Index contour line: every fifth contour line is usually a different (bolder) color.
  • IR: infrared. The near IR clearly differentiates land, water, and vegetation, and is widely used in remote sensing.
  • kb: kilobyte, or one thousand bytes (actually 1024 because the computer uses the binary system).
  • Mac: not spoken here.
  • MaxInt: largest value (32,767) for the standard two byte integer type; used for missing values.
  • Mb: megabyte, or one million bytes (actually 1024²).
  • MICROFIX: a souped up Apple II that ran the Army's first fielded microcompuer terrain analysis program, TERRANAL.
  • MS-DOS: Microsoft Disk Operating System, used by IBM PC and compatibles.
  • MSS: Multi Spectral Scanner, 79 m resolution scanner on the Landsat satellites.
  • NAD-27: North American Datum 1927, datum used for USGS 1:24K DEM and currently published topographic maps. Being replaced by NAD-83.
  • NAD-83: North American Datum 1983, to be adopted imminently. Coordinates (latitude and longitude) will be displaced by up to 100 m from their previous locations under NAD-27.
  • NCIC: National Cartographic Information Center, part of USGS, your source for information on DEMs.
  • NGA: National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (see NIMA, DMA, and AMS)
  • NGDC: National Geophysical Data Center, in Boulder, CO, part of NOAA.
  • NIMA: National Imagery and Mapping Agency, the new name for DMA, until the new name became NGA.
  • NOAA: National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration
  • NT: as in Windows NT, the more secure version of Windows 95, a 32 bit operating system
  • Olenellus: a genus of Lower Cambrian trilobite.
  • PC: personal computer.
  • Pentium: see 80586. Get one.
  • RAM: Random Access Memory, the memory available to the computer to load programs or data.
  • RPF: the NGA data standard for CIB and CADRG.
  • ROM: read only memory, not directly useful to user.
  • Row: runs across (horizontal) a data set; the number of rows measures the vertical (up-down, north-south) extent of a data set.
  • Sexium: the logical next step after the Pentium. But they came out with the Pentium II instead.
  • SLAR: side looking airborne radar.
  • SPOT: commercial French satellite with 10 m resolution.
  • SVGA: super VGA, with 256 or even 32K or more colors in useful resolutions like 1024x768.
  • TEC: the Army's Topographic Engineering Center, at Fort Belvoir, VA. Was once ETL.
  • Terra Base: the last microcomputer DEM manipulation program written at the CGL, USMA, and then maintained after a fashion by ETL. It used 30' subsets of DTED level 1. It was a late 1980’s vintage MS-DOS program.
  • Terra Base II: a military version of MICRODEM fielded in the summer of 1987 by the Army Engineer School for Windows 95/NT.
  • TERRANAL: the first microcomputer DEM manipulation program written at the CGL, USMA. It ran on the Microfix (a souped up Apple II) and on MS-DOS machines. Its author left the Army to raise trilobites, but wound up at USNA. It stands for TERRain ANALyis, nothing dirty, so clean out your mouth with soap and stop being Freudian.
  • TM: Thematic Mapper, 30 m resolution data from Landsat satellites.
  • Turbo Pascal: (archaic) the original language for learning to program.
  • USGS: United States Geological Survey.
  • USMA: United States Military Academy, West Point, NY. The Corps really has.
  • USNA: United States Naval Academy, Annapolis, MD.
  • UTM: Universal Transverse Mercator coordinate system. See Department of the Army (1969) or Merrill (1986).
  • VGA: (archaic) virtual graphics adapter, providing 640x480 resolution in 16 colors or a very grainy 320x200 resolution in 256 colors, selected from a palette of 64 cubed. Once really cool, now you’re in trouble if this is all your computer does.
  • WGS-72: World Geodetic System 1972, datum used for early 1:250K DEM.
  • WGS-84: World Geodetic System 1984, datum now used by DMA.
  • Windows 3.xx: (archaic) nifty operating system, but used enough memory that I didn't use to recommend using it with MICRODEM. Then along came Delphi and cheap memory.
  • Windows 95: great when Delphi 95 came along. MICRODEM now works on nothing else (OK, it works on NT and 98).
