GIS Programs

Long ago, GIS and remote sensing were very different types of programs.  Data came only on large 9 track tapes, and first PCs had limited capabilities and its was very hard to get data loaded.  As time went on, the programs expanded in capabily and developed many overlapping functions.  They also broadened to converge in part in computed-assisted design, with engineering programs like AutoCad, and with building inventory management (BIM) software.  This history means that different terms have be applied to the same concepts, and that even such things as coordinates are not standardized--for example satellite images generally start coordinates in the upper left, with x increasing to the right and y down, while most GIS data like DEMs starts in the lower left with a standard Cartesian grid.

For many people, GIS means ArcGIS like the Microsoft products mean word processors, spreadsheets, and presentation.  ESRI makes ArcGIS (historically there have been other products, like ArcView and ArcInfo, and components like ArcCatalog which have been coupled loosely or tightly integrated), and while ESRI once stood for something, it's now just ESRI.  Like Microsoft, ESRI has bought some standardization to the GIS arena, for example shapefiles, but has also championed proprietary formats like Geodatabases or zLAS which competes with the open standard LAZ for lidar data.

If you rely on open data formats, all the programs will produce very similar results for the most common GIS operations.  If you need specialized operations, your choices might be limited to whichever program can do what you need.  But with the common formats like shapefiles, Geotiffs, and LAS, you can take data into any of the programs, and export the results to any of the others.

This will focus on MICRODEM, because I think:

  • It has a slightly easier interface.
  • It has a number of options for teaching, to show things to help understand the principles behind the program.
  • It has some specialized options for geology, physical geography, and oceanography.

Once you understand the principles of data and GIS operations, you should be able to adapt to a different program.  Note that all the programs evolve, and knowing one today may not help in the next version, so you need flexibility.

GIS libraries and command line programs

  • MICRODEM can call a number of external programs, which are widely used in the GIS community.
  • You may need to use GDAL or LAStools  for your project.  When picking a project, consult the instructor on data issues, and request assistance if needed.
