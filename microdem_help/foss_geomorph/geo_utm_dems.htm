Geographic versus UTM DEMs

A number of free DEMs (e.g. SRTM, ASTER GDEM, USGS NED) all use geographic coordinates because they are "seamless" and can be merged across UTM zone boundaries.  A smart program would compensate, and correctly compute slopes and aspect, hillshades, and viewsheds by converted the decimal degrees into meters with an adjustment for earth curvature.  The default map would also account for the different spacings in X and Y.  With rare exceptions, this is not the case, and you must reproject the geographic DEMs to get reasonable results in most GIS programs.

The area of a grid cell projected onto a UTM map at 60 degrees north, in Alaska.  The 1" geographic spacing is frequently given as 30, but  in the case the 1" pixels are actually about 15x30 m, and the DEM has twice the number of points compared to the true 30 m data set.

In addition to the pixels having different sizes, the relationship changes over the area of the map, with the points in the two grids sometimes being close, and other times being shifted.

The reprojection solutions takes the measured elevations, at the intersections of the blue geographic graticule, and interpolates new elevations at a different number of locations.  The original elevations are then not used.  This will tend to slightly smooth the DEM, as the highest and lowest values will be gone after interpolation.  There is no theoretical reason why reinterpolation should improve the quality or accuracy of a DEM; the algorithm can only attempt to minimize the changes.

The comparison must also consider whether to two grids share a common pixel definition.

Slope algorithms for arc second DEMs.  Only some software correctly handles geodesic slope computations,  without reprojection to UTM.  The option provided by many of the other programs, to use a single scaling factor from degrees to meters, does not account for the difference in data spacing with latitude.
