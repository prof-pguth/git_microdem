Landsat Thermal Computations

Landsat 8 and 9 measure the TOA radiance in the two thermal bands. Band 10 is generally better.

Landsat DN computations--radiance, reflectance, brightness temperature

After converting the DN to radiance, the TB (brightness temperature) can be computed:

  •  TB = K2 / ln(K1/Lλ  + 1)
    • TB = Top of atmosphere brightness temperature (K)
    • Lλ = TOA spectral radiance (Watts/( m2 * srad * μm))
    • K1 = Band-specific thermal conversion constant from the metadata (K1_CONSTANT_BAND_x, where x is the thermal band number)
    • K2 = Band-specific thermal conversion constant from the metadata (K2_CONSTANT_BAND_x, where x is the thermal band number)

If the emissivity is known or can be assumed, the TLS (temperature land surface) can be computed:

  • TLS = TB / ( 1 + lambda * 1e-6 * TB / 1.4388e-2 * ln(Emissivity))
    • lambda is the wavelength at the center of the band in nm
    • 1e-6 converts to m
    • TB is the brightness temperature
    • The constant combines Planck's constant, the Boltzmann constant, and the speed of light (basic physics)
    • This comes from the Stefan-Boltzmann law 

The Landsat thermal bands have an actual spatial resolution of 100 m, but they are reported at 30 m resolution to match the other bands.  They are interpolated, which causes the obvious generalization and lack of detail.  At the observational scale, the earth will not resemble the pristine materials measured in the lab (Table 1).  Rocks will be weathered and partially covered by vegetation, so on the scale seen by Landsat they will have much larger emissivities (Tables 2 and 3).
