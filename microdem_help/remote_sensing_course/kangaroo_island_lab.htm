Lab 3--Kangaroo Island

In the fires in Australia at the beginning of 2020, the unique ecological area of Kangaroo Island was devastated.  Much of the news focused on the lovable dunnart.

We are going to assess the damage from the fires using Sentinel-2 imagery.  The island is on two tiles, PA (west) and QA (east, because in MGRS the first letter is the east-west coordinate, and the second is the north-south coordinate; the 53H gives the 6 degree UTM zone).  You will do the analysis on PA. You can download and uncompress the data from the File, Remote sensing lab menu choice.  There are also three dates, before, during, and after the fire.

Deliverables.  For each of these you probably need at least a short paragraph and a few images.

  1. Write a short introduction, with a map showing the unitiated person where this is.  Say clearly what data you used.
  2. Comment on whether the visible, false color, NBR or NDVI best shows what has burned.  What spectral bands are involved, and why do they work?
  3. Comment on dNBR and dNDVI grids, and what they show, and if they improve on the NBR and NDVI.
  4. Compute the area burned, and the percentage of the island within the PA tile that burned.  Insure that you mask the water, and consider just the land.
  5. Dscuss what band combination best shows the active fire, its wavelength, and what black body temperature emits to give a peak at that wavelenght.  Include a graph.
  6. Have an acknowledgments section, to discss any help you received and give credit.
  • Open image.  For the Sentinel-2 imagery, you want to use the open by directory option.
    • Band selection and  contrast enhancement
  • MSI band combinations to show active fire.  The page lists three band suggestions for OLI on TM, but you should be able to find the corresponding Sentinel-2 bands.  The one reference for Sentinel-2 is for the wavelength, which you can convert to band number. Landsat/Sentinel-2 bands.
  • Create one of the normalized difference indexes
    • NDVI--in addition to vegetation, this can highlight water or barren land
      • NDVI are created with Create new band.
      • Use defined palette display for NDVI  (this should be automatic; lowest colors in gray will be water, then barren land, and finally vegetation in shades of green
      • dNDVI grids are created with  Multiple grid arithmetic,  Difference between two grids and show the difference in vegetation between two scenes (could be seasonal pattern, drought, fire, increased farming) 
      • dNDVI does not have a special palette.
    • NBR and dNBR
      • NBR  created with Create new band.
      • There is no special palette for NBR, so you can use any of the color scales
      • dNBR grids are created with  Multiple grid arithmetic,  Difference between two grids. 
      • For dNBR use defined palette display
  • You should save the computed grids, with a name that lets you know which is which, and somewhere where you can find them.  The program will suggest reasonable names, although file names cannot use some of the math operators.
  • If you only need to work with part of the map area,  File, Save DEM/grid,  Current subset, MD DEM.  This will be smaller, but more importantly, process much faster.
  • If you edit these grids, the changes are only to the copy of the grid in memory.  If you make a mistake, you can reload the DEM from the copy on disk, with an option on the Edit menu.
  • Masking out the water.  Save the intermediate grids in case you want to recreate a step.
    • Find a grid that does a good job seperating the island from the ocean.  You could use:
      • A single band of the satellite.  If you want this, you must Create a new band  with the reflectances for that band.
      • Band ratio
      • Normalized satellite indexes; there is a water index NDWI and you can check the vegetation index NDVI which also clearly differentiates the water
    • Create a mask from the grid.  You can design the mask so you identify the water, or the non-water.  You will give it a value; the rest of the grid will be 0.
    • If the data is noisy, you might do a median Filter on it, which will eliminate single pixels or small groups of pixels.  Try a size of 5.
    • Turn the values in the mask you do not want to missing data. Edit, Mark as missing, All above or all below the value you want.
    • Mask the NBR or NDVI grid with the water mask you just created: Edit, Mark as missing, Open grid.  Pick either the valid points or the missing points in the mask to set missing in your primary grid.
  • If you had a high resolution vector coastline, you could mask with that.  We are not considering that option here because you need practice identifying water from the satellite imagery.
  • Computing areas from the grids
    • Insure you have the NDVI or dNBR color set for the map.  Display Parameter, Elevation map, Fixed color palette, and pick one of the dNBR palettes.  They have slightly different categories, and it does not matter which you use.
    • Subset & zoom and zoom to just the area you want (in this case, to avoid the mainland to the north of Kangaroo Island).
    • Right click on map, Popup legends  to get a database with the categories and the pixel count in each.
      • To get a prettier version, Report, Legend on the database toolbar.
      • To export it to compute areas, Export the legend database with Report, Export HTML, Single table.  You can take the table from the web page, paste it into Excel, and do computations.
